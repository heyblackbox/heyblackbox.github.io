Fonoteka

The simplest and the cheapest music app with a focus on albums listening.

I have designed this app all the way from initial idea to release. It is one of my favorite projects, and I think we made something new here.

During iterations of design and development, we had to step back a few times to refine the logic, shave off excessive features and ideas, leaving only the essential function.

I enjoyed designing a physical metaphor of the interface. It uses inertia and weight to make interactions playful and material.

Fonoteka is not like another streaming music app. Put simply, it does just one thing — give users access to several offline synced albums.

Unbundling music

There are many possible interactions with music or around it. People connect with music in many different ways.

The mission of Fonoteka is to reveal the importance of music album. It's made for those who treat music seriously and prefer albums to automated streams. For those who see music as art, not entertainment.

A first mobile application made at Zvooq music service, Fonoteka was intended to be a part of a family of apps. In this ecosystem, each app should be focused on a particular type of interaction. Just like Facebook did it with its mobile application, Messenger and Paper. After Fonoteka, we have built Zvooq mobile app, which targeted another audience and was different in many ways.

User behavior

To research the area, I observed how people around me listen to music, and interviewed my colleagues and friends. I reflected on my connection with music. We shared ideas with the product team. First insights revealed:

  • During a daily commute, there's not much time and attention for music discovery. Discovering good music takes time and mental effort. In a mobile context, it's more important to have something known to be good.
  • Some people who are seriously into music prefer albums to automated streams of recommended content or celebrity playlists.
  • For any of those listeners, there's normally a "personal album chart" of a few current favorites. This personal chart is slowly changing day to day or week to week, but rarely all at once.
  • Glitches and interruptions during streaming via mobile network are very annoying.

Real world references

Remeber CD changer in a car? To load new music CDs, one should have to go around the car, open the trunk and insert previously selected CDs into the tray. An awkward pattern, but there was a good side: once the album is in the changer, you will likely have a certain connection to it.

This connection to music is not there with pop music charts, automated radio stations, gigabytes of ripped mp3s and most modern streaming services.

Car CD changers of the 90s, commuters in Moscow metro

The question was: how to give the feeling of real, tangible music to daily commuters, who don't have time and attention to select music when they have time to listen.

UX concepts

First: drop the streaming, listen only to synced music. The connections are unreliable.

Second: limited small number of offline albums. Many options make it hard to choose. We keep the "personal album chart" synced, while the larger collection can wait in the cloud. This limitation allowed us to negotiate the lowest subscription price possible.

Third: Ease of use and minimum clutter of the interface. Remember the walkman? You could operate it with one hand inside your pocket.

Designing home screen album interactions

The home screen provided access to saved albums and a way to load new music. I wanted interactions to feel realistic, to support the value of album. Gestures were a way to do it, they are natural actions and don't clutter the UI.

Rejected sketch: dragging

First I tried to use dragging to load album from the cloud, so it would feel like you put a tape into a walkman. It was OK for selecting music , but I could not figure out how to use those slots in the bottom for casual playback.

In the next concept I extended the space outside the screen in all directions.

Second concept: horizontally swipable album cards, up and down swipes for album actions

Left and right swipe gestures were used for selecting a slot. This way we could always have a nice big album cover in the middle of the screen. Up and down swipes fit to "Play" and "Load/Replace" actions.

Based on paper sketches, I created interactive prototypes in Axure and Flinto, pitched them to product and devs teams. Fine tuned animations in Flash to provide a reference for developers.

Key features

  • No streaming, instead “borrow” up to 5 albums per day to listen offline.
  • Limited number of slots to put albums — “CD changer on-the-go”.
  • Select albums from your collection on Zvooq music service, or search the catalog.
  • Buttonless gesture-driven interface.
  • $1 monthly subscription: a few times cheaper than full-featured services.

Improvement

Based on user feedback, we updated the app after the soft-launch:

Improved discovery for new users. The app worked nicely for existing users who had a collection of music from web service. For new users it offered only a search field to select new music. So we added a row of horizontally swipeable recommendations, to make it easier for new users to start.

One price tier instead of two. Initially, there were two price tiers in the app: 3 slots for $1/month and 7 slots for $2 for those who want mor then 3 slots. After we tested the app with live subscribers, we understood that two tiers create more questions than answers. We switched to one tier: 5 slots for $1/month with auto-renewable subscription, which seemed a better balance overall and more value for money.

Personal review

Similar to Zvooq web, advanced music listeners were the core audience of Fonoteka. We got positive feedback about app function and interface interactions.

What we missed: I wish we have worked more on proper positioning and marketing, explaining benefits such as minimalistic approach and low price. The first user session missed proper onboarding scenarios. We had a basic onboarding slideshow, but I think that was not enough. The interface in simple but not straightforward, and educating first time users could have been done better.

Later, the service pivoted its business model towards mainstream audience. We released a full-featured mobile app, Zvooq Mobile, which featured freemium monetization and more expensive paid subscription.

Fonoteka app got more than 600K installs, 2nd place in Music in Russian App Store, 1st place in all apps in 7 countries: Ukraine, Belarus, Kazakhstan, Moldova, Kyrgyzstan, Turkmenistan, Tajikistan.
