Designing a scheduling system for a ridepooling service



Summary

  • As a functional design leader, I have created solutions for and delivered a technically complex functionality (spanning across 4 parts of the product), which effectively enabled the company to run large-scale operations and unlocked new markets.
  • I have researched the problem space, collecting insights from customer interviews, live operation observations, passenger surveys, and bringing them to internal workshops and stakeholder feedback sessions.
  • Mapped interconnected task flows of 4 user types
  • Got validation and approval for a 3-phased design concept that prioritised pressing problems first and offered strategies for the future.
  • Provided guidance, support and adjustments during development iterations.

Context

Ability to plan operations was a feature highly demanded by public transit companies: it would allow them to automate operations from the management side. Without planned operations passengers could not pre-book trips, which had strategic importance for scaling the business.

The scheduling was supposed to add a new dimension — time — to a previously "realtime" system, changing core userflows, and affecting nearly all interfaces of the product. A dedicated cross-team task force was established for implementation, with me tasked as its design leader.

My task

Leading the process from the design side, enable cross-functional teams in the company, arriving to a solution that would make scheduling possible, while meeting the requirements, constraints and deadlines.

Research

I have conducted a series of initial interviews with public transit operators in 4 cities and regions of Germany. The results were brought to internal ideation sessions and then again to customer feedback interviews (there were more than 10 iterations).

The difficulty was in synthesizing a variety of requirements and refining the concept to be both technically viable and a robust foundation for future expansion.

Solution

The concept that made everyone agree was a multi-phased plan which was set up to address pressing problems first and contained design proposalf for possible additional valuable functonality, such as automated schedule generation and dynamic supply-demand matching.

Under my design leadership the team built a planning feature, allowing for larger scale operations, and activated pre-booking capability for passengers, which was a key for important business cases such as commuting or medical transport.

Functional dependencies.


Planning a schedule.


Viewing planned driver shifts in realtime monitoring UI.


Passenger prebooking a ride.


Recovery userflows for handling unplanned changes.
