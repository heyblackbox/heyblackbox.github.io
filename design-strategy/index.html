Applying design methods to product strategy


Summary

  • As the company (ridepooling service) was looking for new business opportunities, I have developed multiple R&D streams, enabling prioritisation of those potential directions and informing changes to product strategy.
  • In the frame of that R&D work, I have explored new problem spaces and created design concepts, focusing on their strategic value, perspectives and dependencies.
  • Being able to see the problem and the solution concept together with its stategic value and dependencies, made it possible to optimize and plan for maximum ROI.

Situation

Are we going to start doing feature X now? It does not look like a #1 priority according to what the users say, but features Y and Z depend on it. And in three months we are going to really need those Y and Z. Aren't we?

Every product leader knows such challenges of strategic prioritisation, where not only the direct value of a feature matters, but also strategic value that is contained in possible dependencies. We were also struggling with it while building a ridepooling service at door2door.

My task

Essentially, my task was to create concepts from vaguely shaped problems, expose the potential business value of those solutions based on user journeys and known targets, and then bring them to a meeting to define priorities for the future.

In the context of a ridepooling service, for which I was doing this job, there could be questions like: "How can we begin supporting autonomous vehicles?", "How users without smatrphones can book rides?" or "Does it make sense to automate charging of electric vehicles during the operation?"

Actions

To develop a concept, I collaborated with domain experts, feature squads and their designers, technical leads and key developers, and external stakeholders. The process was technically close to design sprint, as I set a time bracket for each step, and collected input from a workgroup on the set-up and feedback phases:

  • Explore the problem space, understand industry capability and perspectives
  • Understand existing technical limitations and implications, collect ideas (expert 1:1s or a group session)
  • Develop user flows for possible solutions, understand wins and limitations in terms of UX
  • Get expert feedback, evaluate dependencies in terms of potential value: collect future capabilities that will be unlocked by doing this feature, sort in order of importance
  • Prepare a “pitch deck” that may also include a clickthrough prototype, userflow or a journey map

To prioritise, we used different methods, typically either Impact/effort matrix, or a RICE model. Once the decision was made, I handed the selected concept over to a feature squad. Usually the feature team was already well aware of it, since I involved them upfront as experts for the phase of concept development.

Results

This strategic strategic design work I have done: a comprehensive view of both the problem and the solution concept, along with understanding of its potential value and dependencies, enabled optimization and strategic planning for achieving maximum return on investment.
